Chapter 11: High Availability

High availability is not a feature you bolt onto a database system after the fact — it is a design commitment you make before the first byte of production data is written. This chapter covers what high availability actually means for PostgreSQL and SQL Server environments, how each platform approaches the problem architecturally, and how to build, monitor, and maintain HA systems that survive the failures you expect and the ones you don't. By the end, you will understand not just how to configure these systems, but why they work the way they do and where they break down under pressure.

---

What High Availability Actually Means

The term "high availability" gets used loosely, and that looseness causes real problems when architects and DBAs talk past each other during planning. High availability means the system remains operational and accessible to applications despite failures — hardware failures, network partitions, software crashes, or planned maintenance windows. It is measured as a percentage of uptime, and those percentages matter enormously at scale.

Four nines of availability — 99.99% — sounds impressive until you realize it permits roughly 52 minutes of downtime per year. Five nines, 99.999%, allows only about 5 minutes. When you are working on a system processing financial transactions or healthcare records around the clock, those numbers translate directly into business impact, regulatory exposure, and sometimes contractual SLA penalties.

The core distinction every DBA must internalize is the difference between RTO and RPO. Recovery Time Objective is how long the business can tolerate an outage — the maximum time from failure detection to restoration of service. Recovery Point Objective is how much data loss is acceptable — the maximum age of the data that must be recovered. A system with an RTO of 30 seconds and an RPO of zero is fundamentally different in its architecture than one with an RTO of 4 hours and an RPO of 1 hour. Both can be called "high availability," but they require completely different solutions and carry completely different costs.

HA also exists on a spectrum that includes related but distinct concepts. Fault tolerance means the system continues operating without interruption during a component failure — think of RAID disk arrays continuing to serve reads and writes through a drive failure. Disaster recovery addresses larger-scale events: data center fires, regional outages, catastrophic data corruption. Business continuity is the broader umbrella that includes people, processes, and systems. As a DBA, your domain is primarily the database layer of this stack, but you need to understand where your responsibilities end and where the infrastructure, application, and operations teams pick up.

PostgreSQL and SQL Server approach HA differently because of their architectural histories. PostgreSQL is a single-primary system at its core — writes go to one node and are replicated to standbys. SQL Server grew up in a Windows-first, cluster-aware environment and has deeper integration with the Windows Server Failover Clustering (WSFC) infrastructure. Both platforms have mature HA solutions, but they make different tradeoffs, and understanding those tradeoffs is what separates a DBA who configures HA from one who truly operates it.

Replication Foundations: How Data Gets to Standbys

Before configuring any HA solution, you need to understand how replication moves data from a primary to a standby, because every HA technology in both platforms is built on replication. The mechanism underneath determines your RPO, your failover characteristics, and your performance overhead.

PostgreSQL uses Write-Ahead Logging (WAL) as the foundation for all replication. Every change made to the database — inserts, updates, deletes, DDL — is first written to the WAL before it is applied to the heap files. WAL is an ordered, sequential log. Streaming replication sends this WAL stream from the primary to one or more standby servers in near real time. The standby continuously applies WAL records, keeping its data files in sync with the primary. This is called physical replication because the standby is a byte-for-byte copy of the primary — same data files, same tablespace layout, same PostgreSQL major version.

SQL Server's equivalent is transaction log shipping and log-based replication, but the more sophisticated HA mechanisms use Always On Availability Groups, which also rely on the transaction log. Every write in SQL Server goes through the transaction log first. In an Availability Group, log records are sent from the primary replica to secondary replicas, which apply them in order. The log transport mechanism is similar in spirit to PostgreSQL streaming replication, but the implementation and configuration surface are quite different.

Replication can be synchronous or asynchronous, and this choice is the single most important performance and durability tradeoff in HA design. With synchronous replication, a transaction on the primary does not commit until at least one standby has confirmed it has received and written (and in some modes, applied) the WAL or log records for that transaction. This guarantees zero data loss on failover — your RPO is zero — but it adds latency to every write, because the primary must wait for the round-trip acknowledgment from the standby over the network.

With asynchronous replication, the primary commits immediately after writing locally. The WAL is shipped to standbys in the background, and there is always a lag — however small — between the primary and the standby. On failover, you risk losing the transactions that were committed on the primary but not yet shipped. For many workloads, asynchronous replication with a very low lag is acceptable; for financial systems where every transaction must be durable on multiple nodes, synchronous is the only honest option.

You can monitor replication lag in both systems to understand your actual exposure at any moment.

PostgreSQL:

-- Check replication lag on the primary
SELECT
    client_addr,
    application_name,
    state,
    sent_lsn,
    write_lsn,
    flush_lsn,
    replay_lsn,
    (sent_lsn - replay_lsn) AS replication_lag_bytes,
    write_lag,
    flush_lag,
    replay_lag,
    sync_state
FROM pg_stat_replication
ORDER BY replay_lag DESC NULLS LAST;

SQL Server:

-- Check replication lag on the primary replica
SELECT
    ag.name AS ag_name,
    ar.replica_server_name,
    drs.database_id,
    DB_NAME(drs.database_id) AS database_name,
    drs.synchronization_state_desc,
    drs.synchronization_health_desc,
    drs.log_send_queue_size,
    drs.log_send_rate,
    drs.redo_queue_size,
    drs.redo_rate,
    drs.last_commit_time,
    drs.last_redone_time,
    DATEDIFF(SECOND, drs.last_redone_time, drs.last_commit_time) AS lag_seconds
FROM sys.dm_hadr_database_replica_states drs
JOIN sys.availability_replicas ar ON drs.replica_id = ar.replica_id
JOIN sys.availability_groups ag ON ar.group_id = ag.group_id
WHERE drs.is_local = 0
ORDER BY lag_seconds DESC;

The write_lag, flush_lag, and replay_lag columns in PostgreSQL's pg_stat_replication tell you different things. Write lag is the time from when a WAL record was sent until the standby confirmed writing it to its WAL files. Flush lag adds the requirement that the standby flushed it to durable storage. Replay lag is the time until the standby actually applied the change to its data files. For synchronous standbys, you care most about flush lag — that is what the primary waits for before committing. For asynchronous standbys, replay lag tells you how stale the standby's data is.

One subtlety that catches DBAs off guard in PostgreSQL: replication lag is not just about network bandwidth. The standby can fall behind because it cannot apply WAL records fast enough — this happens when the standby is under heavy query load (read replicas serving reports, for example) and the WAL apply process is competing for I/O. This is a performance problem, not a network problem, and the fix is different.

PostgreSQL Streaming Replication and Patroni

Setting up PostgreSQL streaming replication manually is educational, but running it manually in production is a liability. Without automation, you need a human to detect a primary failure, verify it, decide to promote a standby, perform the promotion, and redirect application connections. Even with a very fast and well-trained team, that process takes minutes. Patroni eliminates most of that response time by automating the decision and action.

To understand how the automation works, you first need to understand the manual process. A standby server runs in recovery mode — it is continuously applying WAL from the primary and is read-accessible (with hot_standby = on) but cannot accept writes. If you need to promote it to primary, you call pg_promote() in PostgreSQL 12+ or create a recovery.signal promotion trigger file in older versions. After promotion, the server exits recovery mode, becomes writable, and begins generating its own WAL.

PostgreSQL:

-- On the standby: check if this server is in recovery (standby) mode
SELECT pg_is_in_recovery();

-- On the primary: see the current WAL insert position
SELECT pg_current_wal_lsn();

-- On the standby: see the last WAL location received and replayed
SELECT
    pg_last_wal_receive_lsn() AS received,
    pg_last_wal_replay_lsn()  AS replayed,
    pg_is_in_recovery()       AS is_standby;

The problem with manual promotion is split-brain: if the primary is actually alive but temporarily unreachable (a network partition rather than a hardware failure), and you promote a standby, you now have two nodes that both believe they are the primary and both accepting writes. Reconciling that state is painful and potentially involves data loss. This is why automated HA systems use a distributed consensus mechanism to avoid split-brain.

Patroni uses etcd, Consul, or ZooKeeper as a Distributed Configuration Store (DCS). The primary continuously writes a "leader lock" to the DCS with a short TTL. As long as the primary is healthy, it refreshes that lock before it expires. If the primary fails, the lock expires, and one of the standbys — whichever wins the race to acquire the new leader lock — promotes itself. Because only one node can hold the lock at a time (the DCS enforces this with atomic compare-and-swap operations), split-brain is prevented at the consensus layer.

Patroni also manages pg_hba.conf and postgresql.conf across the cluster, handles cascading replication, and exposes a REST API and patronictl command-line tool that you use to perform controlled switchovers, check cluster state, and manage configuration changes. A patronictl switchover initiates a graceful handoff: the current primary verifies the target standby is fully caught up, the primary stops accepting new writes, waits for the standby to apply remaining WAL, and then promotes the standby. The old primary joins the cluster as a standby. The whole process takes a few seconds in a healthy cluster with synchronous replication.

What Patroni does not do is redirect application connections automatically. For that, you need a proxy layer. PgBouncer with a configuration management hook, HAProxy health-checking Patroni's REST API, or pgpool-II are common choices. HAProxy is widely used because it can poll http://patroni-node:8008/ — if a node is the current leader, the API returns HTTP 200; standbys return HTTP 503. HAProxy routes write traffic only to the leader and can route read traffic to any healthy node.

SQL Server Always On Availability Groups

SQL Server's primary HA technology is Always On Availability Groups (AGs), introduced in SQL Server 2012 and significantly matured since. An AG is a set of user databases — the availability group — that fail over together as a unit. The AG has one primary replica that accepts all writes and up to eight secondary replicas in SQL Server 2019+ (four in earlier versions). Secondary replicas can serve read-only workloads if you configure readable secondaries.

The AG sits on top of Windows Server Failover Clustering (WSFC), which provides the cluster membership, heartbeat monitoring, and failover orchestration. Each node in the cluster runs SQL Server, and the WSFC monitors node health. When the WSFC determines the primary node is unhealthy, it triggers a failover according to the AG's failover policy. In SQL Server 2017 and later, there is also a Linux-based clustering option using Pacemaker, which follows a similar conceptual model.

An AG has a listener — a virtual network name and IP address that floats to whichever node is currently the primary replica. Applications connect to the listener name and are transparently routed to the current primary. After a failover, the listener IP moves to the new primary, and applications reconnect to the same name without needing a configuration change. This is the SQL Server equivalent of HAProxy routing to the Patroni leader, but it is built into the platform and managed by the cluster infrastructure.

You can query the AG state from any replica to understand the health of the system.

SQL Server:

-- View the overall health of all availability groups on this instance
SELECT
    ag.name                          AS availability_group,
    ags.primary_replica,
    ags.synchronization_health_desc,
    ags.primary_recovery_health_desc,
    ar.replica_server_name,
    ar.availability_mode_desc,
    ar.failover_mode_desc,
    drs.synchronization_state_desc,
    drs.log_send_queue_size          AS unsent_log_kb,
    drs.redo_queue_size              AS unapplied_log_kb
FROM sys.availability_groups ag
JOIN sys.dm_hadr_availability_group_states ags
    ON ag.group_id = ags.group_id
JOIN sys.availability_replicas ar
    ON ag.group_id = ar.group_id
JOIN sys.dm_hadr_database_replica_states drs
    ON ar.replica_id = drs.replica_id
WHERE drs.is_local = 0
ORDER BY ag.name, ar.replica_server_name;

PostgreSQL:

-- Equivalent view of replication state in PostgreSQL
-- Run on the primary to see all connected standbys
SELECT
    application_name,
    client_addr,
    state,
    sync_state,
    sent_lsn,
    replay_lsn,
    (sent_lsn - replay_lsn)  AS lag_bytes,
    replay_lag
FROM pg_stat_replication
ORDER BY replay_lag DESC NULLS LAST;

Always On AGs support three failover modes: automatic failover, manual failover, and forced failover with potential data loss. Automatic failover requires synchronous-commit mode on the target secondary and is the configuration you want for maximum HA. Manual failover is used for planned maintenance — you initiate it deliberately, and SQL Server ensures the secondary is fully synchronized before the failover completes. Forced failover is the emergency option when the primary is truly gone and you need to bring a secondary online even if it means acknowledging potential data loss.

The synchronous-commit requirement for automatic failover has real performance implications. Every transaction on the primary must wait for the secondary to harden the log record before the commit returns to the application. On a LAN where roundtrip latency is under 1 millisecond, this overhead is often imperceptible. When the secondary is in a different availability zone or data center with 5–20 ms of network latency, every write pays that cost. A common production architecture uses one synchronous secondary in the same data center for automatic failover and one or two asynchronous secondaries in remote data centers for disaster recovery, accepting that the DR failover carries some data loss risk.

SQL Server 2019 introduced distributed availability groups, which allow you to link two separate AGs together — useful when you want DR replication between clusters that cannot share the same WSFC infrastructure, such as two separate cloud regions or two on-premises data centers with separate Active Directory forests.

One feature worth understanding that has no direct PostgreSQL equivalent is the readable secondary with read-intent routing. You can configure the AG listener to route connections that specify ApplicationIntent=ReadOnly in their connection string to a secondary replica automatically. This is useful for offloading reporting queries, SSRS subscriptions, or analytics workloads without changing application code significantly. The tradeoff is that data on the secondary may be slightly stale in asynchronous mode, and queries on the secondary can block WAL redo on very long-running read queries, which creates a feedback loop that increases lag.

Failover, Fencing, and the Mechanics of Promotion

Failover is the moment every HA system is designed for, and it is also the moment when the gaps in your design reveal themselves. Understanding the exact sequence of events during a failover — and where it can go wrong — is what separates an HA system you can trust from one you hope will work.

In any HA system, the cluster manager must answer one question before promoting a standby: is the primary truly dead, or is it merely unreachable? A primary that is unreachable but still running and accepting writes is dangerous — promoting a standby in that scenario creates split-brain. The mechanism that prevents this is called fencing, also known as STONITH (Shoot The Other Node In The Head). Fencing forcibly isolates or kills the old primary before the standby is promoted. In Pacemaker-based clusters, this typically means sending an out-of-band power cycle command through IPMI or iLO, or revoking a SCSI reservation on shared storage. In cloud environments, it might mean terminating the instance or detaching its network interface.

In Patroni, the DCS lock mechanism provides the fencing guarantee. When the old primary cannot refresh its leader lock — because it crashed, lost network connectivity, or is running too slowly — the lock expires. At that moment, the old primary loses the ability to write to the DCS and therefore loses the right to be primary. If it comes back up and finds that another node holds the leader lock, it will not attempt to serve writes; it will demote itself and start replicating from the new primary. The DCS is the single source of truth about who the leader is, and Patroni trusts it unconditionally.

In SQL Server Always On with WSFC, the cluster uses a quorum model to determine whether a failover should proceed. The cluster needs a quorum of votes to form. Each node in the cluster typically gets one vote, and there may be a disk witness or a cloud witness that acts as a tiebreaker. If the primary node fails and the remaining nodes have quorum, the cluster can proceed with failover. If a network partition splits the cluster such that neither partition has quorum, both sides remain passive — this prevents split-brain at the cost of availability. The quorum configuration is one of the most important and most commonly misconfigured aspects of WSFC-based HA.

The promotion sequence in PostgreSQL streaming replication with Patroni looks like this in practice: the primary's health check fails, Patroni on all standby nodes detects the leader lock has expired (or is about to), each standby races to acquire the lock in the DCS, one wins, that node calls pg_promote(), PostgreSQL exits recovery mode on that node, Patroni updates the DCS with the new leader's connection information, and HAProxy or whatever proxy is in front routes new connections to the new primary. Applications using persistent connections will experience an interruption — their existing connections to the old primary are dead. Applications using connection pooling with retry logic will reconnect quickly. Applications without retry logic will fail until they are restarted.

In SQL Server, the equivalent sequence involves the WSFC detecting node failure, achieving quorum among remaining nodes, selecting the designated automatic failover partner, SQL Server on the secondary completing any outstanding log redo if it is in synchronous mode (or acknowledging data loss if forced), bringing the AG databases online in primary role on the new node, and moving the listener IP to the new node. Applications connected to the listener name will experience a brief interruption as DNS or the cluster network name updates, but reconnect to the same listener name.

The key operational insight is that applications must be designed for failover, not just databases. A database failover takes seconds in a well-configured system, but if the application holds a transaction open, doesn't retry on connection error, or caches a resolved IP address for the database connection, the effective outage is much longer. As a DBA, you often cannot control application code, but you can set expectations and document the failover behavior clearly so that application teams design for it.

Connection Management, Read Replicas, and Geographic Distribution

Production HA architectures are rarely just one primary and one standby. Real systems layer multiple capabilities: read scale-out through replicas, geographic distribution for disaster recovery, and connection management to route traffic intelligently. Understanding how to design and operate these layered architectures is where HA moves from configuration into engineering.

Read replicas — standbys configured for read-only access — are one of the most effective ways to scale read-heavy workloads. In PostgreSQL, any standby with hot_standby = on can accept SELECT queries. In SQL Server, readable secondaries in an AG serve the same purpose. The architectural win is that read replicas are essentially free from a data perspective — the replication cost exists anyway for HA, and allowing reads on the standby costs you only the compute and I/O on the standby node.

The catch is read consistency. A query on an asynchronous replica might return data that is seconds or minutes behind the primary. For some queries — analytical reports, search indexes, audit logs — that lag is acceptable. For other queries — a user refreshing their account balance immediately after a transaction — stale data is a problem. Application teams need to make deliberate routing decisions: write to primary, read from replica for eventually-consistent reads, read from primary for strongly-consistent reads. This is a data architecture conversation, not a database configuration conversation, and DBAs need to drive it rather than wait for it to happen organically.

In PostgreSQL, you can check whether a session is running on a replica and what the current replication lag is from inside application queries.

PostgreSQL:

-- Application can call this to check if it is connected to a primary or replica
-- and estimate data freshness before running a lag-sensitive query
SELECT
    pg_is_in_recovery()                                AS is_replica,
    pg_last_wal_replay_lsn()                           AS last_applied_lsn,
    now() - pg_last_xact_replay_timestamp()            AS replay_lag,
    CASE
        WHEN pg_is_in_recovery() THEN 'replica'
        ELSE 'primary'
    END AS role;

SQL Server:

-- Check replica role and lag from within a session on a secondary
SELECT
    DATABASEPROPERTYEX(DB_NAME(), 'Updateability') AS db_updateability,
    ars.role_desc                                  AS replica_role,
    drs.synchronization_state_desc,
    drs.last_commit_time,
    drs.last_redone_time,
    DATEDIFF(MILLISECOND,
        drs.last_redone_time,
        drs.last_commit_time)                      AS estimated_lag_ms
FROM sys.dm_hadr_availability_replica_states ars
JOIN sys.dm_hadr_database_replica_states drs
    ON ars.replica_id = drs.replica_id
WHERE drs.is_local = 1;

Geographic distribution adds another dimension. When your standby is in a different region — across the country or in a different cloud availability zone — network latency makes synchronous replication impractical for all writes. A 60 ms round-trip to your DR site means every write from the application pays 60 ms of added latency if replication is synchronous. Most architectures solve this with a cascade replication topology: the primary replicates synchronously to a local standby (which provides zero-RPO automatic failover), and that local standby replicates asynchronously to the remote DR site (which provides eventual recovery with some data loss risk but no write latency penalty).

In PostgreSQL, cascading replication is configured by having one standby connect as a replication source for another standby. The intermediate standby generates no primary WAL — it passes through the WAL stream from the primary — so it works transparently.

In SQL Server, distributed AGs serve this purpose in multi-datacenter deployments. Within a single data center, you configure one AG with synchronous replicas for HA. That AG then participates in a distributed AG with a second AG in the remote data center, with asynchronous replication between them. The distributed AG allows each site to have its own cluster infrastructure while maintaining a log-based replication relationship between them.

One operational discipline that is underestimated in HA systems is connection pooling. Every time a failover occurs, all persistent connections to the old primary become invalid. If your application opens 200 connections to the database, all 200 will simultaneously attempt to reconnect to the new primary the moment they detect the failure. This thundering herd of reconnections can overwhelm a newly-promoted primary that is still catching up on its workload.
