Chapter 36: Storage Tiers: gp2 vs gp3 vs io1 vs io2

Storage decisions that took minutes on-premises — "provision a 2TB LUN from the SAN, set stripe size to 64KB, done" — become architecture decisions in AWS RDS with lasting cost and performance consequences. The volume type you select at instance creation determines not just your baseline throughput and IOPS ceiling, but also how you scale, what you pay, and whether you can migrate away without downtime. This chapter breaks down the four RDS storage types — gp2, gp3, io1, and io2 — from the perspective of a DBA who already understands disk I/O mechanics and just needs to know how AWS maps those mechanics onto managed storage. You will learn when to use each tier, how to configure them correctly via CLI and Terraform, how to avoid the gp2 burst credit trap that degrades production performance silently, and how to make the right call for OLTP, analytics, and mixed workloads.

---

Understanding the Storage Landscape: What Replaced Your SAN

On-premises, you controlled every layer: disk spindle count, RAID level, controller cache, queue depth, stripe alignment. RDS abstracts all of that, but the underlying physics still apply. What AWS gives you is a set of pre-tuned abstractions backed by EBS (Elastic Block Store), and your job is to pick the right abstraction for your workload pattern.

The four volume types map loosely to familiar concepts:

| Volume Type | On-Premises Analog | Best For |

|---|---|---|

| gp2 | Mid-range SAN with burst tier | Dev, test, small OLTP |

| gp3 | Performance SAN with flat pricing | Most production workloads |

| io1 | All-flash array, dedicated IOPS | I/O-intensive OLTP |

| io2 | All-flash array, enterprise durability | Mission-critical, financial systems |

Key distinction before anything else: gp2 and gp3 are "general purpose" SSDs. io1 and io2 are "provisioned IOPS" SSDs. The naming matters because it controls what guarantees AWS makes about latency and IOPS consistency. General purpose volumes can throttle. Provisioned IOPS volumes guarantee sub-millisecond latency at the configured IOPS level.

The one fact that changes everything: gp2 ties IOPS to storage size. gp3 decouples them. This single architectural difference makes gp3 almost always the better default for new provisioning in 2024.

#### gp2: The Legacy Default You Should Stop Using

gp2 allocates 3 IOPS per GB of storage, with a minimum of 100 IOPS and a maximum of 16,000 IOPS. That ceiling hits at 5,334 GB (approximately 5.3 TB). Beyond that, you get no additional IOPS regardless of how much storage you add.

The burst mechanism works exactly like EC2 T-series CPU burst credits. Volumes under 1 TB accumulate burst credits allowing 3,000 IOPS for short periods. When credits are exhausted, performance drops to the baseline 3 IOPS/GB. A 100 GB gp2 volume has a baseline of 300 IOPS. During credit burst it can hit 3,000 IOPS. When the credits run out — which happens after roughly 30 minutes of sustained 3,000 IOPS on a 100 GB volume — performance collapses to 300 IOPS. Applications do not receive warnings. CloudWatch metrics will show BurstBalance declining, but unless you have alarms configured, you will discover the problem when your query times triple at 2 AM.

The burst credit math:

Credit balance: starts at 5,400,000 (enough for 30 minutes at 3,000 IOPS)
Credit earn rate: baseline_IOPS * 1 credit/second
Credit consumption: (actual_IOPS - baseline_IOPS) * 1 credit/second

For a 200 GB gp2 volume (600 IOPS baseline):
- Burst IOPS available: 3,000
- Consumption rate at full burst: 2,400 credits/second
- Depletion time: 5,400,000 / 2,400 = 37.5 minutes

For volumes over 1 TB (baseline > 3,000 IOPS), the burst mechanism no longer applies because the baseline already meets or exceeds 3,000 IOPS. This is why many teams accidentally run fine on gp2 for years — their storage grew large enough to push past the burst ceiling before they noticed.

#### gp3: The Right Default for Most Production Workloads

gp3 decouples IOPS from storage size entirely. Every gp3 volume starts with 3,000 IOPS and 125 MB/s throughput included in the base price, regardless of volume size. You can provision up to 16,000 IOPS and 1,000 MB/s independently, paying only for what you configure above the baseline.

This matters practically: a 100 GB gp3 volume gets the same guaranteed 3,000 IOPS as a 10 TB gp3 volume at base pricing. No burst credits, no performance collapse, no minimum storage floor to hit an IOPS target.

Pricing comparison as of 2024 (us-east-1, approximate):

| Volume Type | Storage Cost | IOPS Cost | Throughput Cost |

|---|---|---|---|

| gp2 | $0.115/GB-month | Included (tied to size) | Included |

| gp3 | $0.092/GB-month | 3,000 free, then $0.020/IOPS | 125 MB/s free, then $0.040/MB/s |

| io1 | $0.125/GB-month | $0.065/IOPS | Included |

| io2 | $0.125/GB-month | $0.065/IOPS (first 32K) | Included |

The storage cost alone makes gp3 cheaper per GB than gp2. On a 500 GB volume needing 6,000 IOPS:

  • → gp2: requires 2,000 GB to reach 6,000 IOPS → $230/month just for storage
  • → gp3: 500 GB + 3,000 additional IOPS → $46 storage + $60 IOPS = $106/month

    • That is a roughly 54% cost reduction for the same performance.

    io1 vs io2: When You Need Guaranteed IOPS

    Provisioned IOPS volumes exist for workloads where gp3's 16,000 IOPS ceiling is insufficient, or where you need single-digit millisecond latency guarantees that you are willing to pay for explicitly.

    #### io1: The Workhorse Provisioned Tier

    io1 allows up to 64,000 IOPS per volume (on Nitro-based instances) and up to 1,000 MB/s throughput. The IOPS-to-storage ratio constraint is critical: you can provision a maximum of 50 IOPS per GB of storage. A volume needing 64,000 IOPS requires at minimum 1,280 GB of storage, even if your actual data fits in 200 GB. This is the io1 "storage tax" — you pay for storage you do not need just to satisfy the ratio constraint.

    io1 durability is 99.8–99.9% annual failure rate (between 0.1% and 0.2% chance of data loss per year). This sounds low, but io2 improves it dramatically.

    #### io2: io1's Successor with Better Economics

    io2 offers 99.999% durability (100x more durable than io1), the same IOPS ceiling of 64,000 per volume, and a more favorable IOPS-to-storage ratio of 500 IOPS per GB (10x better than io1). The 500:1 ratio means that same 64,000 IOPS volume only requires 128 GB of storage rather than 1,280 GB, eliminating most of the storage tax.

    Pricing between io1 and io2 is identical at $0.065/IOPS, making io2 strictly better than io1 for new provisioning. AWS has not officially deprecated io1, but the industry direction is clear: io2 is the recommended provisioned tier.

    io2 Block Express (available on R5b, X2idn, X2iedn, and newer instance families) extends the ceiling further: 256,000 IOPS, 4,000 MB/s throughput, and sub-millisecond P99 latency. For extreme OLTP workloads — financial transaction systems, high-frequency trading audit logs, real-time fraud detection — this is the tier to evaluate.

    When provisioned IOPS actually makes sense:

  • → You need more than 16,000 IOPS (gp3 ceiling)
  • → Your workload has consistent I/O patterns and latency sensitivity that gp3's shared infrastructure cannot guarantee
  • → You have a mixed read/write ratio with concurrent I/O from dozens of connections
  • → Regulatory requirements mandate documented performance guarantees (some compliance frameworks require it)

    • When provisioned IOPS is overkill:

  • → Workloads with high average IOPS but acceptable burst latency
  • → Development and staging environments
  • → Read-heavy workloads with caching layers (ElastiCache, RDS read replicas) absorbing the majority of reads
  • → Any workload under 16,000 sustained IOPS

    Configuring Storage: CLI, Terraform, and Modification Patterns

    #### Creating Instances with Specific Storage Types

    # Create a production PostgreSQL RDS instance with gp3 storage
# Note: --iops and --storage-throughput only apply to gp3/io1/io2
aws rds create-db-instance \
  --db-instance-identifier prod-postgres-01 \
  --db-instance-class db.r6g.2xlarge \
  --engine postgres \
  --engine-version 15.4 \
  --master-username dbadmin \
  --master-user-password "$(aws secretsmanager get-secret-value \
    --secret-id prod/rds/master-password \
    --query SecretString \
    --output text)" \
  --allocated-storage 500 \
  --storage-type gp3 \
  --iops 6000 \
  --storage-throughput 500 \
  --storage-encrypted \
  --kms-key-id arn:aws:kms:us-east-1:123456789012:key/mrk-abc123 \
  --vpc-security-group-ids sg-0abc123def456789 \
  --db-subnet-group-name prod-db-subnet-group \
  --backup-retention-period 7 \
  --preferred-backup-window "03:00-04:00" \
  --preferred-maintenance-window "sun:05:00-sun:06:00" \
  --deletion-protection \
  --region us-east-1

# Create an io2 instance for high-IOPS OLTP workload
aws rds create-db-instance \
  --db-instance-identifier prod-sqlserver-oltp \
  --db-instance-class db.r6i.4xlarge \
  --engine sqlserver-ee \
  --engine-version 15.00.4312.2.v1 \
  --master-username sa \
  --master-user-password "${SA_PASSWORD}" \
  --allocated-storage 2000 \
  --storage-type io2 \
  --iops 64000 \
  --storage-encrypted \
  --license-model license-included \
  --region us-east-1
    # Modify an existing gp2 instance to gp3
# This is the most common migration task — no downtime required
aws rds modify-db-instance \
  --db-instance-identifier legacy-postgres-gp2 \
  --storage-type gp3 \
  --iops 6000 \
  --storage-throughput 500 \
  --apply-immediately

# Monitor the modification status
aws rds describe-db-instances \
  --db-instance-identifier legacy-postgres-gp2 \
  --query 'DBInstances[0].{
    Status:DBInstanceStatus,
    StorageType:StorageType,
    IOPS:Iops,
    Throughput:StorageThroughput,
    PendingModified:PendingModifiedValues
  }' \
  --output table

# Check the pending modified values before apply-immediately
aws rds describe-db-instances \
  --db-instance-identifier legacy-postgres-gp2 \
  --query 'DBInstances[0].PendingModifiedValues'
    # Monitor gp2 burst balance — set this alarm before any production gp2 usage
aws cloudwatch put-metric-alarm \
  --alarm-name "RDS-BurstBalance-Low-legacy-postgres" \
  --alarm-description "gp2 burst balance below 30% — performance degradation imminent" \
  --metric-name BurstBalance \
  --namespace AWS/RDS \
  --statistic Average \
  --dimensions Name=DBInstanceIdentifier,Value=legacy-postgres-gp2 \
  --period 300 \
  --evaluation-periods 2 \
  --threshold 30 \
  --comparison-operator LessThanThreshold \
  --alarm-actions arn:aws:sns:us-east-1:123456789012:rds-alerts \
  --ok-actions arn:aws:sns:us-east-1:123456789012:rds-alerts \
  --treat-missing-data missing

# Also alarm on IOPS hitting the provisioned limit
aws cloudwatch put-metric-alarm \
  --alarm-name "RDS-IOPSNearLimit-prod-postgres" \
  --alarm-description "IOPS exceeding 90% of provisioned capacity" \
  --metric-name ReadIOPS \
  --namespace AWS/RDS \
  --statistic Average \
  --dimensions Name=DBInstanceIdentifier,Value=prod-postgres-01 \
  --period 60 \
  --evaluation-periods 5 \
  --threshold 5400 \
  --comparison-operator GreaterThanThreshold \
  --alarm-actions arn:aws:sns:us-east-1:123456789012:rds-alerts

    #### Terraform Configuration for Storage Tiers

    # terraform/modules/rds/main.tf
# Production-grade RDS module with parameterized storage configuration

variable "storage_config" {
  description = "Storage configuration for the RDS instance"
  type = object({
    type              = string
    allocated_gb      = number
    iops              = optional(number)
    throughput_mbps   = optional(number)
    max_allocated_gb  = optional(number)  # enables autoscaling
  })

  validation {
    condition = contains(["gp2", "gp3", "io1", "io2"], var.storage_config.type)
    error_message = "storage_config.type must be one of: gp2, gp3, io1, io2"
  }

  validation {
    condition = !(var.storage_config.type == "gp2" && var.storage_config.iops != null)
    error_message = "gp2 does not support explicit IOPS configuration"
  }

  validation {
    condition = !(
      contains(["io1", "io2"], var.storage_config.type) &&
      var.storage_config.iops == null
    )
    error_message = "io1 and io2 require explicit IOPS configuration"
  }
}

locals {
  # io1 max ratio: 50 IOPS/GB; io2 max ratio: 500 IOPS/GB
  max_iops_ratio = {
    io1 = 50
    io2 = 500
    gp3 = 500  # gp3 allows up to 500 IOPS/GB above 3000 baseline
    gp2 = 3    # implicit, not configurable
  }

  # Validate io1/io2 IOPS-to-storage ratio
  iops_to_storage_ratio = (
    var.storage_config.iops != null ?
    var.storage_config.iops / var.storage_config.allocated_gb :
    0
  )
}

resource "aws_db_instance" "this" {
  identifier     = var.db_identifier
  engine         = var.engine
  engine_version = var.engine_version
  instance_class = var.instance_class

  # Storage configuration
  allocated_storage     = var.storage_config.allocated_gb
  max_allocated_storage = var.storage_config.max_allocated_gb  # null disables autoscaling
  storage_type          = var.storage_config.type
  iops                  = var.storage_config.iops
  storage_throughput    = var.storage_config.throughput_mbps  # gp3 only

  storage_encrypted = true
  kms_key_id        = var.kms_key_arn

  # Prevent accidental deletion
  deletion_protection       = var.environment == "prod"
  skip_final_snapshot       = var.environment != "prod"
  final_snapshot_identifier = var.environment == "prod" ? "${var.db_identifier}-final" : null

  tags = merge(var.common_tags, {
    StorageTier     = var.storage_config.type
    ProvisionedIOPS = tostring(coalesce(var.storage_config.iops, 0))
  })
}

# Outputs for downstream monitoring configuration
output "storage_details" {
  value = {
    type         = aws_db_instance.this.storage_type
    allocated_gb = aws_db_instance.this.allocated_storage
    iops         = aws_db_instance.this.iops
    throughput   = aws_db_instance.this.storage_throughput
    arn          = aws_db_instance.this.arn
  }
}

    Storage Autoscaling, Modification Limits, and the Gotchas Nobody Tells You

    #### Storage Can Only Grow

    This is the most important operational constraint in RDS storage management: you cannot shrink allocated storage. Once you provision 2 TB, you are paying for 2 TB forever on that instance. The only escape is a snapshot restore to a new instance with smaller storage, which requires downtime or a blue-green deployment.

    The practical consequence: start conservatively and use autoscaling.

    # Enable storage autoscaling on an existing instance
# max-allocated-storage must be at least 10% larger than allocated-storage
aws rds modify-db-instance \
  --db-instance-identifier prod-postgres-01 \
  --max-allocated-storage 2000 \
  --apply-immediately

# Disable autoscaling by setting max equal to current allocated (not below)
aws rds modify-db-instance \
  --db-instance-identifier prod-postgres-01 \
  --max-allocated-storage 500 \
  --apply-immediately

    RDS autoscaling triggers when: free storage falls below 10% of allocated storage AND remains there for 5 minutes, AND at least 6 hours have passed since the last autoscale event. It increases storage in increments of either 5 GB or 10% of current allocation, whichever is larger. It will not scale above max-allocated-storage.

    The 6-hour cooldown is a real operational risk. If your database has a runaway load that consumes storage at 1 GB/minute, autoscaling at the 5-minute threshold fires once, adds 50 GB, and then is locked for 6 hours while storage continues filling. The right pattern is autoscaling plus a CloudWatch alarm on free storage at the 15% threshold so you have warning before autoscaling even activates.

    #### The Modification Window Problem

    Storage type changes (e.g., gp2 to gp3) use --apply-immediately, but storage size increases do not always complete immediately. A storage modification on a running instance triggers an optimization phase that AWS calls "storage optimization" — this is essentially an online resize. Depending on volume size, this can take minutes to hours. During optimization, the instance remains available, but:

  • → You cannot trigger another storage modification
  • → The instance shows status `storage-optimization` in `describe-db-instances`
  • → Failover times for Multi-AZ may increase slightly during optimization
    • # Check if a storage modification is still in progress before attempting another
aws rds describe-db-instances \
  --db-instance-identifier prod-postgres-01 \
  --query 'DBInstances[0].{
    Status:DBInstanceStatus,
    PendingValues:PendingModifiedValues,
    StorageType:StorageType,
    AllocatedStorage:AllocatedStorage,
    IOPS:Iops
  }'

# Example output when optimization is in progress:
# {
#   "Status": "storage-optimization",
#   "PendingValues": {},
#   "StorageType": "gp3",
#   "AllocatedStorage": 1000,
#   "IOPS": 6000
# }

    #### Multi-AZ and Storage Modifications

    For Multi-AZ instances, storage modifications are applied to the standby first, then the primary. This means:

    1. AWS modifies the standby EBS volume

    2. A failover occurs (brief interruption, typically under 60 seconds)

    3. The former standby (now primary) runs on the new storage

    4. The former primary (now standby) gets its storage updated

    Applications connected with proper retry logic will reconnect automatically. Connection poolers like RDS Proxy absorb the failover without the application seeing it. If you are connecting directly to the RDS endpoint without retry logic, that 60-second window causes errors.

    This also means --apply-immediately does not mean "instantly" for Multi-AZ instances — it means "during this maintenance event" rather than "at next maintenance window," but the actual modification still requires the failover cycle.

    Benchmarking Your Storage Tier Before Migrating Production

    Before migrating a workload from on-premises or from one storage tier to another, establish a baseline. This requires understanding what I/O profile your database actually has — sequential vs. random, read vs. write, queue depth.
