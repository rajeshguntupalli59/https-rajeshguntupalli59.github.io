Chapter 12: Disaster Recovery

Databases fail. Hardware dies without warning, human error wipes out tables, ransomware encrypts your data volumes, and entire data centers go offline. Disaster recovery is the discipline that determines whether your organization survives those events or doesn't. This chapter covers how to think about recovery, how to build the mechanisms that make it possible, and how to test and operate those mechanisms so that when the moment comes — and it will come — you are not improvising. Both PostgreSQL and SQL Server have mature, battle-tested tools for this work, and understanding both systems side by side gives you the mental model to design and defend recovery strategies across any environment.

---

Defining Recovery: RPO, RTO, and What You Are Actually Protecting

Before configuring a single backup job or replication stream, you need to know what you are protecting and what the business expects if things go wrong. Two terms govern every disaster recovery conversation: Recovery Point Objective (RPO) and Recovery Time Objective (RTO).

RPO is the maximum amount of data loss the business can tolerate, expressed as time. An RPO of four hours means the organization accepts losing up to four hours of transactions. An RPO of zero means no data loss is acceptable at any point — which is achievable but expensive. RPO drives how frequently you back up and whether you need synchronous replication.

RTO is the maximum amount of time the business can be down before the impact becomes unacceptable. An RTO of two hours means you have two hours from the moment of failure to restore service. RTO drives your recovery architecture — whether you need a warm standby already running, a cold backup that takes hours to restore, or a hot standby that can take over in seconds.

These two numbers are not technical decisions. They come from the business, finance, legal, and operations stakeholders who understand the cost of downtime and the cost of data loss. Your job as a DBA is to translate those numbers into technical architecture and then honestly tell the business what each architecture costs. A one-minute RTO with zero RPO requires always-on synchronous replication across geographically separated nodes. A four-hour RTO with a twenty-four-hour RPO can be satisfied with nightly backups restored to a spare server. The gap between what the business wants and what it is willing to pay for is a conversation you should initiate before a disaster, not after.

Beyond RPO and RTO, identify what you are protecting:

  • → **The data itself** — transactions, rows, files, BLOBs
  • → **The schema and configuration** — table definitions, indexes, stored procedures, roles, permissions
  • → **The environment** — server configuration, extension libraries, linked server definitions, agent jobs, custom scripts

    • Many recovery failures happen because DBAs successfully restore the data but lose critical configuration that was never backed up. Build a complete picture of everything your database depends on.

    Backup Strategies: Physical vs. Logical, Full vs. Incremental

    There are two fundamental ways to capture the state of a database: physical backups and logical backups. Each has a role, and production environments typically use both.

    Physical backups copy the raw data files as the database engine stores them on disk. They are fast to create for large databases, fast to restore, and support point-in-time recovery when combined with transaction log backups. The tradeoff is that physical backups are generally not portable across major versions and require the same platform or compatible storage to restore.

    Logical backups export the data in a human-readable or SQL-based format. They are portable, useful for moving data between versions or platforms, and can be selectively restored at the table or row level. The tradeoff is that they are slow to create and restore for large databases and do not natively support point-in-time recovery.

    In PostgreSQL, pg_dump is the primary logical backup tool, while pg_basebackup is the physical backup tool. In SQL Server, native backups (BACKUP DATABASE) are physical, and bcp, SSIS, or BACPAC exports are logical equivalents.

    #### Full Backups

    A full backup captures the entire database at a point in time. It is self-contained: to restore, you need only the full backup file. Full backups form the foundation of every recovery strategy, but they are expensive in time and storage when run frequently against large databases.

    #### Differential and Incremental Backups

    Differential backups (SQL Server) capture all changes since the last full backup. They grow over time and are faster to restore than a long chain of incremental backups. Incremental backups (PostgreSQL WAL-based, using tools like pgBackRest or Barman) capture only changes since the last incremental or full backup, minimizing storage but requiring the full chain during restore.

    #### Transaction Log Backups

    This is where point-in-time recovery lives. By continuously capturing transaction logs (PostgreSQL: WAL segments; SQL Server: transaction log backups), you can restore a database to any specific moment between backup events. This is the mechanism that lets you say: "Restore to exactly 2:47:23 PM, before the developer dropped the table."

    PostgreSQL:

    -- Initiate a base backup using pg_basebackup
-- Run this from the OS, not inside psql:
-- pg_basebackup -h localhost -U replication_user -D /backup/base -Ft -z -Wal -P

-- Inside psql: check WAL archiving status
SELECT pg_walfile_name(pg_current_wal_lsn()) AS current_wal_file,
       archived_count,
       last_archived_wal,
       last_archived_time,
       failed_count,
       last_failed_wal,
       last_failed_time
FROM pg_stat_archiver;

    SQL Server:

    -- Full backup
BACKUP DATABASE [AdventureWorks]
TO DISK = N'E:\Backups\AdventureWorks_full.bak'
WITH COMPRESSION,
     CHECKSUM,
     STATS = 10,
     NAME = N'AdventureWorks Full Backup';

-- Differential backup
BACKUP DATABASE [AdventureWorks]
TO DISK = N'E:\Backups\AdventureWorks_diff.bak'
WITH DIFFERENTIAL,
     COMPRESSION,
     CHECKSUM,
     STATS = 10;

-- Transaction log backup
BACKUP LOG [AdventureWorks]
TO DISK = N'E:\Backups\AdventureWorks_log.bak'
WITH COMPRESSION,
     CHECKSUM,
     STATS = 10;

    One detail that catches many DBAs off guard in SQL Server: the database must be in FULL or BULK_LOGGED recovery model to take log backups. In SIMPLE recovery model, the transaction log is automatically truncated after each checkpoint, and point-in-time recovery is not possible. Always verify the recovery model before designing your log backup chain.

    SQL Server:

    -- Verify recovery model for all databases
SELECT name,
       recovery_model_desc,
       log_reuse_wait_desc,
       is_auto_shrink_on   -- this should always be 0 in production
FROM sys.databases
WHERE database_id > 4   -- exclude system databases from view
ORDER BY name;

    PostgreSQL:

    -- PostgreSQL equivalent: check WAL configuration
SHOW wal_level;
SHOW archive_mode;
SHOW archive_command;

-- wal_level should be 'replica' or 'logical' for archiving
-- archive_mode should be 'on'
-- archive_command should point to a real destination

    #### Backup Retention and the 3-2-1 Rule

    The 3-2-1 rule is a baseline: keep 3 copies of your data, on 2 different media types, with 1 copy offsite. For production databases, extend this thinking:

  • → Keep at least 30 days of daily full backups plus log chains for OLTP systems
  • → Keep at least 90 days for regulatory environments
  • → Store backups on storage that is logically and physically separate from the primary database server
  • → Encrypt backups at rest — an unencrypted backup file on a compromised server is a breach
  • → Test restoration from offsite copies, not just the local copies

    • Backups that have never been tested are not backups. They are assumptions.

    Point-in-Time Recovery: Mechanics and Execution

    Point-in-time recovery (PITR) is the most frequently used disaster recovery mechanism in production environments. A developer runs DROP TABLE orders; at 3:12 PM. You restore the database to 3:11:59 PM. This is the scenario PITR was built for, and understanding its mechanics prepares you to execute under pressure.

    #### PostgreSQL PITR

    PostgreSQL's PITR relies on the WAL (Write-Ahead Log) archive. The process:

    1. Restore a base backup to the target data directory

    2. Create a recovery.signal file in the data directory (PostgreSQL 12+)

    3. Configure recovery_target_time or recovery_target_lsn in postgresql.conf

    4. Start PostgreSQL — it will replay WAL segments up to the target

    The restore_command tells PostgreSQL where to fetch archived WAL segments during recovery. This is the inverse of archive_command.

    A full postgresql.conf recovery block looks like this (added to postgresql.conf before starting):

    restore_command = 'cp /mnt/wal_archive/%f %p'
recovery_target_time = '2024-03-15 15:11:59'
recovery_target_action = 'promote'

    Then create the signal file:

    touch /var/lib/postgresql/data/recovery.signal

    When you start the server, PostgreSQL will read available WAL segments and stop at the specified time. The recovery_target_action = 'promote' instruction tells it to become a writable primary after reaching the target, rather than pausing for inspection.

    PostgreSQL:

    -- After recovery completes, confirm the server timeline and LSN
SELECT pg_current_wal_lsn() AS current_lsn,
       pg_postmaster_start_time() AS server_started,
       current_timestamp AS checked_at;

-- Verify the data is at the right point in time
-- (run a query against the affected table)
SELECT COUNT(*) AS order_count,
       MAX(created_at) AS most_recent_order
FROM orders;

    #### SQL Server PITR

    SQL Server's PITR works through the restore sequence: full backup, then optional differential, then log backups applied in order with STOPAT.

    -- Step 1: Restore the most recent full backup, leaving the database in
-- a restoring state so we can apply subsequent backups
RESTORE DATABASE [AdventureWorks]
FROM DISK = N'E:\Backups\AdventureWorks_full.bak'
WITH NORECOVERY,
     REPLACE,
     STATS = 10;

-- Step 2: Apply the most recent differential backup taken before the target time
RESTORE DATABASE [AdventureWorks]
FROM DISK = N'E:\Backups\AdventureWorks_diff.bak'
WITH NORECOVERY,
     STATS = 10;

-- Step 3: Apply log backups in order with STOPAT
-- Apply all log backups up through the one that contains the target time
RESTORE LOG [AdventureWorks]
FROM DISK = N'E:\Backups\AdventureWorks_log_0900.bak'
WITH NORECOVERY;

RESTORE LOG [AdventureWorks]
FROM DISK = N'E:\Backups\AdventureWorks_log_1200.bak'
WITH NORECOVERY;

-- Final log backup: stop at specific time before the DROP TABLE
RESTORE LOG [AdventureWorks]
FROM DISK = N'E:\Backups\AdventureWorks_log_1500.bak'
WITH RECOVERY,
     STOPAT = '2024-03-15T15:11:59';

    Notice that every backup except the final one uses NORECOVERY. This keeps the database in restoring mode so subsequent backups can be applied. The last restore uses RECOVERY, which brings the database online. If you accidentally use RECOVERY on an intermediate step, the database comes online at that point and subsequent log backups cannot be applied — you have to start over.

    The tail-log backup is a detail that saves many recoveries. If your primary database is still accessible at the moment of disaster (for example, a corruption in one table, not a total server loss), take a tail-log backup before beginning the restore sequence. This captures any transactions committed after the last scheduled log backup, shrinking your data loss window.

    SQL Server:

    -- Take a tail-log backup of the damaged database
-- This requires NO_TRUNCATE if the database is in a damaged state
BACKUP LOG [AdventureWorks]
TO DISK = N'E:\Backups\AdventureWorks_taillog.bak'
WITH NO_TRUNCATE,
     NORECOVERY,
     COMPRESSION,
     CHECKSUM;

    After this tail-log backup, you can include it as the final step in your restore sequence and lose zero transactions.

    High Availability vs. Disaster Recovery: Standbys, Replication, and Failover

    High availability (HA) and disaster recovery (DR) are related but distinct. HA addresses failures within a single site — a server crash, a disk failure, a network card dying. DR addresses site-level failures — an entire data center going offline, a fire, a flood, a regional network outage. Good architectures address both, and the tools for each overlap significantly.

    #### PostgreSQL Streaming Replication

    PostgreSQL's native streaming replication sends WAL data from a primary to one or more standby servers in near real-time. The standby applies the WAL continuously, staying within seconds of the primary. This serves both HA (failover within the same data center) and DR (standby in a different region).

    Key configuration on the primary (postgresql.conf):

    wal_level = replica
max_wal_senders = 5
wal_keep_size = 512MB    # keep 512MB of WAL segments on primary

    On the standby, postgresql.conf is identical to the primary (it was copied during pg_basebackup), but a standby.signal file is placed in the data directory, and the recovery source is configured:

    primary_conninfo = 'host=primary_host port=5432 user=replication_user password=secret'
primary_slot_name = 'standby1_slot'  # replication slot keeps WAL from being removed

    Synchronous vs. Asynchronous Replication is the critical architectural choice. With asynchronous replication (default), the primary commits the transaction and notifies the client immediately, then ships the WAL to standbys. This gives excellent write performance but means the standby might be slightly behind if the primary fails — potential data loss within that lag window. With synchronous replication, the primary waits for at least one standby to confirm WAL receipt before acknowledging the commit. Zero data loss, but write latency increases by the round-trip time between primary and standby.

    # Synchronous standby configuration on primary
synchronous_standby_names = 'FIRST 1 (standby1, standby2)'

    PostgreSQL:

    -- Monitor replication lag from the primary
SELECT client_addr,
       application_name,
       state,
       sent_lsn,
       write_lsn,
       flush_lsn,
       replay_lsn,
       (sent_lsn - replay_lsn) AS replication_lag_bytes,
       write_lag,
       flush_lag,
       replay_lag,
       sync_state
FROM pg_stat_replication
ORDER BY application_name;

    SQL Server:

    -- Monitor Always On AG synchronization state
SELECT ag.name AS ag_name,
       ar.replica_server_name,
       ars.role_desc,
       ars.synchronization_health_desc,
       ars.connected_state_desc,
       adbrs.database_name,
       adbrs.synchronization_state_desc,
       adbrs.log_send_queue_size,
       adbrs.redo_queue_size,
       adbrs.last_commit_time
FROM sys.availability_groups ag
JOIN sys.availability_replicas ar
    ON ag.group_id = ar.group_id
JOIN sys.dm_hadr_availability_replica_states ars
    ON ar.replica_id = ars.replica_id
JOIN sys.dm_hadr_database_replica_states adbrs
    ON ar.replica_id = adbrs.replica_id
ORDER BY ag.name, ar.replica_server_name;

    #### SQL Server Always On Availability Groups

    SQL Server's primary HA and DR technology is Always On Availability Groups (AG). An AG groups one or more databases and replicates them as a unit to secondary replicas. Like PostgreSQL streaming replication, secondaries can be synchronous or asynchronous. Unlike basic streaming replication, AGs include:

  • → A **listener** — a virtual network name and IP address that clients connect to, which redirects to the current primary automatically
  • → **Automatic failover** — when configured with synchronous replicas and a Windows Server Failover Cluster (WSFC) or Pacemaker quorum, the AG can fail over automatically
  • → **Readable secondaries** — read workloads (reporting, analytics, backups) can be offloaded to secondary replicas, reducing primary load

    • For DR specifically, a common pattern is two synchronous replicas within the primary data center (for HA with zero data loss failover) plus one asynchronous replica in a geographically separate region (for DR with a small data loss window acceptable).

    #### Failover Execution

    Failover — whether planned or emergency — follows a different procedure than restore. In a planned failover (maintenance, hardware upgrade), you synchronize data completely, then switch primary responsibility to the standby. In an emergency failover (primary is dead), you promote the standby with whatever data it received up to the moment of failure.

    PostgreSQL — promote a standby:

    # Graceful promote (if primary is still reachable, use planned switchover)
pg_ctl promote -D /var/lib/postgresql/data

# Or using psql trigger function
# SELECT pg_promote();
    -- Confirm promotion succeeded
SELECT pg_is_in_recovery();   -- should return false after promotion
-- Also check pg_stat_replication to verify other standbys are now connected to the new primary

    SQL Server — manual failover of an AG:

    -- Connect to the target secondary replica and initiate failover
-- For planned failover (with data synchronization):
ALTER AVAILABILITY GROUP [ProductionAG] FAILOVER;

-- For forced failover when primary is unreachable (potential data loss):
ALTER AVAILABILITY GROUP [ProductionAG] FORCE_FAILOVER_ALLOW_DATA_LOSS;

    After a forced failover, you must resynchronize and rejoin the old primary as a secondary once it recovers. Skipping this step leaves orphaned replicas and splits the AG. Always document post-failover steps before a disaster, not during one.

    Backup Verification, Corruption Detection, and Restore Testing

    The most common failure mode in disaster recovery is not the disaster itself — it is discovering during recovery that your backups are corrupt, incomplete, or restorable only to a different version. Backup verification is not optional maintenance. It is the mechanism that tells you whether your recovery plan actually works.

    #### Verifying Backup Integrity

    SQL Server's RESTORE VERIFYONLY checks the backup set for completeness and basic integrity without actually restoring the database. It is fast and should be part of every backup job.

    SQL Server:

    -- Verify backup integrity without restoring
RESTORE VERIFYONLY
FROM DISK = N'E:\Backups\AdventureWorks_full.bak'
WITH CHECKSUM;

-- Check backup history and verify recent backups exist
SELECT TOP 10
       bs.database_name,
       bs.backup_start_date,
       bs.backup_finish_date,
       bs.type AS backup_type,      -- D=Full, I=Differential, L=Log
       bs.backup_size / 1048576.0 AS size_mb,
       bs.is_damaged,
       bmf.physical_device_name
FROM msdb.dbo.backupset bs
JOIN msdb.dbo.backupmediafamily bmf
    ON bs.media_set_id = bmf.media_set_id
WHERE bs.database_name = 'AdventureWorks'
ORDER BY bs.backup_start_date DESC;

    For PostgreSQL, pgBackRest has a built-in verification command (pgbackrest verify) that checks backup integrity, confirms WAL segments are in place, and validates that a restore chain is complete. If you use pg_basebackup directly without a tool like pgBackRest or Barman, you need to periodically test restores manually.

    PostgreSQL:

    -- After restoring a backup to a test server, verify data consistency
-- Check for corruption using pg_catalog queries

-- Check for invalid indexes (sign of page-level corruption)
SELECT schemaname,
       tablename,
       indexname,
       idx_scan,
       idx_tup_read,
       idx_tup_fetch
FROM pg_stat_user_indexes
WHERE idx_scan = 0
  AND schemaname NOT IN ('pg_catalog', 'information_schema')
ORDER BY schemaname, tablename;

-- Run VACUUM ANALYZE after a restore to refresh statistics
VACUUM ANALYZE;

-- Check for tables with dead tuples indicating inconsistency
SELECT schemaname,
       relname,
       n_dead_tup,
       n_live_tup,
       last_vacuum,
       last_analyze
FROM pg_stat_user_tables
WHERE n_dead_tup > 1000
ORDER BY n_dead_tup DESC;

    #### Database Consistency Checks

    SQL Server's DBCC CHECKDB is the gold standard for consistency validation. It checks every page in the database for structural integrity, verifies index consistency, and reports corruption down to the object and page level. Run it regularly in production — weekly at minimum, daily for critical systems. It can be resource-intensive, so schedule it during low-traffic windows or run it against a restored copy.

    SQL Server:

    -- Run consistency check with extended logical checks
DBCC CHECKDB ('AdventureWorks')
WITH NO_INFOMSGS,
     ALL_ERRORMSGS,
     EXTENDED_LOGICAL_CHECKS;

-- For very large databases, check individual filegroups on a rolling schedule
DBCC CHECKFILEGROUP ('AdventureWorks', DEFAULT)
WITH NO_INFOMSGS;

-- Check a specific table
DBCC CHECKTABLE ('Sales.Orders')
WITH NO_INFOMSGS;

    PostgreSQL does not have a built-in equivalent to DBCC CHECKDB for running storage-level checks while online. The closest approach combines pg_dump (which reads every page and will fail on corruption) with amcheck extension for index validation.

    PostgreSQL:

    -- Use amcheck extension for index corruption detection
CREATE EXTENSION IF NOT EXISTS amcheck;

-- Check all B-tree indexes in the current database
SELECT nspname || '.' || relname AS index_name,
       bt_index_check(c.oid, true) AS check_result
FROM pg_class c
JOIN pg_namespace n ON c.relnamespace = n.oid
JOIN pg_am am ON c.relam = am.oid
WHERE am.amname = 'btree'
  AND n.nspname NOT IN ('pg_catalog', 'information_schema')
ORDER BY nspname, relname;

-- Note: bt_index_check returns NULL on success; any error message indicates corruption

    #### Regular Restore Testing

    Commit to a restore test schedule and treat it like a production event. A practical schedule for most organizations:

  • → **Monthly**: restore a full backup to an isolated test server, verify row counts and spot-check data, confirm the restore completed within RTO
  • → **Quarterly**: perform a full PITR exercise — simulate a specific type of failure, execute the recovery runbook from scratch, measure actual time to recovery
  • → **Annually**: execute a full DR simulation — fail over to the DR site, validate all applications connect and function, fail back

    • Document every test result: what you restored, when it was taken, how long the restore took, what you verified, and what you found. The pattern across tests tells you whether your RPO and RTO are achievable in practice. When tests reveal gaps — a restore taking twice the RTO, a missing WAL segment, a permission error on the restore account — those are production failures that happened safely in a test environment.
